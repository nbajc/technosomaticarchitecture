01. Manifesto

The Digital Prophylactic

Modern architecture of the digital realm functions as a technological membrane—what Lacan would recognize as the ultimate objet petit a: the object-cause of desire that can never be possessed, only endlessly approached. The Digital Prophylactic is the primary infrastructure of contemporary intimacy; a sterile sanctuary engineered for connection that guarantees, by its very structure, that the Real of the other will never be encountered.

The Economy of the Phantom

Žižek argues that ideology functions most effectively when we know it is ideology—and still act as though we do not. The digital marketplace of desire operates identically: we know it is simulation, and swipe anyway. Swipe. Trade pics. Negotiate worth. In this symbolic economy, chemistry is replaced by convenience, intimacy by mere access. The subject discovers not connection but its own lack, reflected back as infinite scroll. Everyone is horny; no one is human.

02. Taxonomy
I.

The Phantom Emotion

The Pseudo-Partner

I document the ‘teleportation’ of intimacy between individuals who have spent years constructing an alternate reality through screens. In Lacanian terms, the Phantom is a fantasy in the precise sense: a scenario staged to fill the gap in the Other, to make the unbearable structural impossibility of sexual relation feel temporary, contingent, soluble by sufficient bandwidth. The partner is not a ‘loved one’ in any traditional sense, but a persistent projection of shared symbolic habit—a quilting point around which the subject’s desire coalesces without ever touching ground.

II.

The Digital Void

The Sterile Sanctuary

The digital interface constitutes a Void in the strict topological sense Lacan assigns to the torus: a non-place defined entirely by its hole. The shared geography of the digital couple is structurally identical to what Lacan calls the Autre—a location that is always-already barred. This Void is not an absence of space but the wrong kind of presence: a sterile sanctuary that stages connection as its alibi while enforcing, at the level of the Real, a permanent quarantine from the flesh of the other.

III.

The Biological Glitch

Auto-Erotic Devolution

The Biological Glitch names what Žižek calls the return of the Real within the symbolic: the moment the body refuses the clean architecture of mediated desire and asserts its own catastrophic logic. The Technosomatic Homunculus—the cortical map of the other, sedimented through years of digital habit—fires against a sensory vacuum, producing climax without contact. What Lacan called jouissance short-circuits the fantasy and collapses the couple back into the one. It is not failure. It is the system operating exactly as designed.

03. Statement

“Technosomatic Architecture utilizes the female gaze as a clinical lens.”

Theoretical Frame

Where Lacan insists there is no sexual relationship—only the endless, constitutive failure to achieve one—the digital interface promises to solve this impossibility through infrastructure. It cannot. It should not. The gap is not a bug in human sexuality. It is its engine.

Moving away from the voyeuristic “God’s-eye view” of traditional representation, my work investigates the Synthetic Couple as a clinical specimen. By integrating the precision of structural engineering with the nuances of affective computing, I expose how digital mediums function as what Žižek calls the interpassive object: the thing that enjoys on your behalf, so you don’t have to—and in doing so, forecloses the very encounter it simulates.

Through this pro-feminist reclamation of the somatic, I challenge the viewer to confront a reality where the digital interface is not a bridge but a mirror—and where we are never more alone than when we are “together.” This is not a tragedy to be corrected. It is the structural condition of subjectivity that the screen, at last, makes visible.
